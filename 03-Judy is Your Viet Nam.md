Naevius the Poet in the Salon of Junia Gabinii

*translated by M. Odessa Asp*

a note from the translator:

*When I was approached to undertake a prose translation of one of the elegies of Quintus Naevius Poeta, I was immensely flattered and a little intimidated.  I’m not a translator by trade.  I find Latin poetry to be beautiful in its own right and that most of my efforts to render it into English invariably suffer from my eagerness to try to recreate the beauty of the original sentiments in some sort of equivalent phrasing.  I call this type of translation “Latinese,” a style which mangles the English language into doing things it really rather would not do and which is probably only intelligible to people who could just as easily have read the Latin themselves.*	  
	*In this project I made a concerted effort to keep the Latinese at bay.  I endeavored, instead, to convey some of my favorite qualities of Naevius’ poetry in a more colloquial English style: his light-heartedness, his playfulness, his wit.  You will find my translation diverges from the original text of the elegy on a lexical, syntactical, and narrative level.  On the lexical level I did not shy away from modern language, fully aware that Naevus would have been wholly unaware of an elevator, much less an elevator pitch, but finding the lexical currency of the latter phrase too precise to Junia’s intent to pass by.  On the syntactical level I have opted for short, clipped sentences and even ungrammatical sentence fragments in order to evoke a lively and personable tone.  On the narrative level I have filled in some details that a modern reader would expect in a short story, including – meā culpā – a cameo by Zenobia of Naevius’ Kraters of Zenobia.  I am fully aware of the division in scholarship concerning whether or not there was ever such a person; please forgive my fleeting flight of fancy.  I also transposed some elements of the narrative where I found it would create a more natural flow in English.  But enough of stalling– enjoy the adventures of Naevius the Poet in the Salon of Junia Gabinii.*

    When I was coming up as a poet the place to be was at Gabinius’ house.  His wife would throw these parties and everyone who was anyone in the poetry scene in Rome had a standing invitation.  It was Junia’s own little personal salon; in there she was host and taste-maker all rolled into one.  And she had amazing taste.  Flaminius Flavus wrote the better part of books two and three of his Satires there; Marinianus had a seat reserved for him that only Decentius ever dared to sit in when the old man couldn’t make it; Zenobia used to look up from the deep meditation of her Kraters and laugh at his jokes.

I don’t know how I scored my first invitation.  I REALLY don’t know how I kept getting invited back.  But I would walk in on my tottery elegies and soon enough my betters were getting busy bettering me.  We spent all night in our cups and our verses, trading lines back and forth, off-the-cuff, sharpening our wits and our wordplay.  We were so comfortable with one another it hardly felt like one-up-manship.  When the water levels were rising up, all our boats went with it.  They were the best nights of my life.

The entry hall to Gabinius’ house may as well have been a temple to the spirits of his distinguished ancestors.  Their sacred images stared at one another across a central water feature, painted busts on two lines of gilt marble plinths, a visual guide to the Gens Gabinia and its former glories.

Junia cultivated as deep and enthusiastic a reverence for these ancestors-in-law as she ever had for her own or those of her first husband.  Possibly even more so.  I could see why; there were some pretty amazing stories she could tell you about them– and she did.  If she ever caught you in that front hall looking at a statue, she would sweep your hand up in hers and recite you a breathless panegyric-cum-elevator-pitch highlighting deeds worthy of immortality in song on top of in marble.  

The closest I ever came to ever taking her up on one of her set topics was the story of Aulus Gabinius.  When he was Consul, he paid out some satisfaction to Clodia – AKA Lesbia, from Catullus’ poems  – by putting his stamp on the law that ended in banishment for the great lawyer Cicero.  If you don’t remember the details of their beef, look them up, sometime, it’s NUTS.  And this same Aulus Gabinius, when he was Praetor, approved Pompey’s expedition to wipe out piracy in the Mediterranean.  I had some notion of a clever line in mind, how under this great man’s watch all in Rome were protected at once from pirates and lawyers.

But for all his achievements, Aulus was not the star of the entry hall.  Across from him, and from a somewhat taller plinth, stared him down the image of Publius Gabinius Secundus Chaucius.  You read that right.  Four names.  Like a fucking hero.  He earned the honorific Chaucius from the god Claudius himself.  Claudius – when he was still just Emperor, and not yet a god – had picked him to lead a military campaign against the Chaucii.  He came back from that campaign holding – I shit you not – the actual last of the lost Eagles stolen by the Germans in the Teutoburg massacre.  Talk about big.  And Junia knew how to treat his image properly.  She had a slave on duty at the door at all times, whose only job was to hand each person entering the house a single flower along with instructions – at least to those who weren’t privy to the tradition yet – to leave it at the great hero’s bust, so that his offerings, like his fame, might never fall from full flower.

Let’s just say there were a lot of flowers involved.

But one Gabinius whose face you’d never see in the entry hall – or anywhere else in the house, as far as I could tell – was Junia’s actual husband.  He was a real serious kind of guy, I always got the impression that he found his wife’s dedication to the arts a little frivolous and maybe even embarrassing.  I thought he must have some sort of secluded study somewhere on the grounds where he could frown at his records-keeping in peace and quiet while we were all over.  I’d go to have a piss and on the way I’d take all these detours trying to suss out the guy’s hiding spot.  I don’t really know why I wanted to meet him.  Just curious, I guess.  But no luck.   I thought, you know, maybe he just went out.  Maybe he had a lover of his own who matched his grump for grump.  Maybe they were frowning intently together while I was pissing in the pot.

Not that I was thinking about him, at all.  I’d end up back in the entry hall; I’d think about that marble Gabinius, instead, the one covered in flowers.  No one could escape having learned about the Teutoburg massacre growing up. The German general Arminius lured Publius Quinctilius Varus and his legions into a dark wood and then signaled an ambush from the shadows when the Roman carts got bogged down in the mud from the rain.  It was ages ago, but left a deep scar on the our empire, a scar shaped like the Rhine and Ister rivers.  

It only took the total slaughter of three legions along with their support staff for the great god Augustus to slap up a heavily fortified border along those rivers and warn against ever crossing them again.  The native German tongue on the further side could live on, safe from the predations of our Latin, which has already mostly wiped out the native languages of Spain and of Gaul. In return, we had some sort of assurance that no second Arminius would come Hannibaling across the Alps or something.  But still, the image of Germans lurking in green shadows cast a long pall over our Roman imagination.

Anyway.

So one night I’m standing there in the entry hall and Junia comes in and is like, “Oh, there you are, I thought you left.”

 “Nah,” I told her, “I just had to have a piss.”

“Have a piss?”  I couldn’t tell whether she was laughing at me or actually grossed out.  “Is that how the old poets say it?”

“You want me to write a poem about having a piss?” I call her out on her teasing.

“I don’t think you could,” she challenged me.  “You’re awfully drunk.”

Oh, I was.  But it didn’t stop me rising to the call and slapping together a few of what I vaguely remember to have been quite stately hexameters on the topic, which, if I don’t reproduce them here, reader, I need you to forgive me.  My brain was a wet marsh and there was no wax tablet at hand to note it down. 

Not to mention that soon enough my drunken tottering found me and her pressed passionately against a column, exchanging more saliva than witticisms while the flower-slave at the door awkwardly tried to find something else to look at.  I think he resorted to deciphering a high-up bit of graffiti in the vestibule with his cheeks puffed out as if he hadn’t already read it a thousand times.

Well, let me tell you, if I had known making out with his wife against a column was the quickest way to meet Gabinius, I’d probably have done it earlier instead of creeping around the back stairs looking for the study.  My eye socket make quick acquaintance with his fist when he appeared fully out of nowhere. As I was promptly escorted from the property, I couldn’t help but make some quip about Junia having an Arminius of her own at home– she’d kept him well-hidden, lured me into a false sense of security, bogged my mind’s wagon wheels down in a mud of wine and good food, and then– bam.

I missed that place.  Junia?  Not really.  But her salon, the food, the wine, the company.  I felt like I was just coming into my own on the poetry scene, and then suddenly I was back in the book shops and smart bars, scrabbling for the latest news, which was probably already three weeks’ stale on the floor of Junia’s formal dining room.

I picked up my spirits, eventually.  Did pretty well on my own, I think.  It was ten years later – one whole siege of Troy – when I heard the news that the house of Gabinius was in mourning, and that they were carrying out the body of its head of household to their family tomb south of town.  I didn’t go, of course, even if I was curious to see Junia with her hair down in her mourning weeds.  I let what I considered a proper number of months pass by and then I went peeking around and tried my luck on getting in.

I really needn’t have worried, it turns out.  The flower-slave had long abandoned his post and no one seemed to have relieved him of his duties. I strolled in unimpeded – and  un-flowered –  and found myself suddenly all wrapped up in Junia’s arms.  She held me like I was her long-lost brother, or son, or whatever.  If she wanted to kiss me, I maintained a certain familial posture and gave her my cheek to kiss, maybe still a little nervous in case the rumors of the funeral were only a lure to get me here so Gabinius could have his way with my face one more time.  

But no, there he was, in marble, alongside his ancestors, keeping a less furtive watch of the entry hall than he had in life.  I wondered what stories Junia would tell of him in the years to come.  His victory over the intoxicated poet would scarcely be material for verse.  No flowers laid at his plinth, I noticed.  And even those strewn before the great Chaucius ranged from wilted and discolored to positively moldy or dried-out.

Junia’s cheeks had kind of lost their flower, too.  She seemed thin and drawn, whether from mourning or age.  When she invited me to stay for salon, though, you know it was an immediate yes.

Let me tell you what I found in the dining room that night.  Some fresh recruits, young hopefuls, all of them sitting on their hands, too scared to recite in case they made a mistake and got called out on it.  A few veterans of the field, but ones who hadn’t published anything in ages – the Muses’ own deserters, still dining out on decades-old laurels.  More eating than talking; more drinking than either– yet no sense that the wine was getting anyone anything other than sleepy.  No sense of camaraderie, of support, of love for the word.  Just some wretched poets sitting wretchedly around hoping not to get called on to present.  
	It couldn’t have been any more depressing than it was for old Chaucius’ men, returning to the Teutoberg after so long to pluck up what bones and remains they could from the site of the slaughter and give them some suitable honors.  What a battlefield Junia made of her salon\!  A proper successor of the Varian Disaster– a Junian Disaster, as it were.  To think that I was almost part of that pile of bones.  Be careful out there, poets.
