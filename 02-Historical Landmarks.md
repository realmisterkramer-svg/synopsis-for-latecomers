**Historical Landmarks**

by

       Sonya Rousseau

     That slick on the driveway asphalt – was it black or yellow? So hard to remember now, and no one thought to check at the time.  
    Nixon, Woodstock, Vietnam, Apollo 11 and the first moon walk – 1969 reverberated chaotically in my 8-year-old head, barely able to keep up. Sometimes the Sunday afternoon family drive felt like the only constant feature, one that spanned my entire young life. Dread would start building in my gut even before I opened my eyes on Sundays, but the drives were non-negotiable.   
     God, Dad loved his brand-new Chrysler Town & Country station wagon, with its woodgrain sides and AM radio that only seemed to get two stations – some religious channel and one that only played oldies. He was always tinkering around with it and even showed me how all the parts worked.   
   Anyway, we’d straggle out to the front driveway each Sunday \- why do I recall every single one of them as having been either blisteringly hot or icy? – and slide into our seats. I was always in the back, middle, stuck between my older brother and sister. No air conditioning, no heat reaching that far back in the winter; one way or another, my legs were either sticking to the vinyl seat or blue with cold. Sis was fine, mostly detached, listening to her transistor radio with an earplug when she could get away with it. But my brother had mastered the fine art of subtle torture, and he saved all his best ideas for those trips. He came up with stuff that I still try not to remember.  
     When I think about it now, the real torture for us was being forced to sit as the audience of our parents’ chilly, awkward silences. Sure, they talked to us. Dad was the tour guide, pointing out monuments, battle grounds, historic buildings, sometimes stopping the car long enough for us to read the markers or poke around a little. Mom occasionally passed out tiny Dixie cups of special-occasion sugary drinks or sawdusty, healthy snacks, or she’d start conversations about school or the neighborhood that died quickly. I don’t know. Maybe they thought it was educational for us kids. Maybe they thought it would help keep the family together.   
     Before Dad packed up the station wagon to move in with his new family at the end of the year, he sat us all down and told us he wanted to continue the Sunday afternoon drive tradition. It was important, he said. And maybe the new siblings could be squeezed into the station wagon, too. One big, happy family.  
     I’m glad that I learned so much from Dad. Knowledge is power, as my third grade teacher always said. No one knows why Dad couldn’t stop his packed station wagon in time on that December day. There were patches of black ice on some stretches of road. Anyway, the car was burned beyond recognition, so there’s no telling.  
     I ended up majoring in history in college. Still can’t get enough of the stuff. Hey, I’m interested in things, go figure. My own son is 8 years old now, and, well, old habits die hard. Sunday afternoons were just made for driving around, right? And there are so many monuments, battle grounds, and historic buildings to explore. We make sure to visit Dad’s monument just about every week, polish the chrome Mom custom-ordered to wrap around the edges of the stone. It’s peaceful there. Sometimes I think I catch a glimmer of something dark swirling in my beautiful son’s eyes, though, and I wonder if maybe I should consider selling the car.
